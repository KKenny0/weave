# Deep-read Lens Variants

Choose a reading lens by the source's reasoning shape and the user's question. Source format is a clue, not the decision: a paper can be primarily conceptual, and an interview can contain a tight causal argument.

Multiple lenses may contribute to a Source Brief, but one should lead. State why the lead lens fits and what it may miss.

## Argument and narrative lens

Use when the source tries to move the reader from an existing answer to a new conclusion.

Extract:

- the concrete problem or tension;
- the default or prior answer being challenged;
- the source's distinctive move;
- claims, evidence, assumptions, and rhetorical transitions;
- the conditions under which the move no longer works.

Use `[bone] / [muscle] / [connective]` paragraph tagging only when the argument is dense enough that structural labeling helps. Do not tag every paragraph by ritual.

Use a witnessed-problem -> prior-route -> new-opening narrative only when all three are present in evidence. For descriptive reports or exploratory interviews, preserve their actual shape.

Blind spot: can over-credit the author with a clean innovation arc and underweight accumulated or distributed causes.

## Academic extraction lens

Use when method, measurement, or empirical support carries the conclusion.

Extract:

- research question and competing baseline;
- operational definitions and measured variables;
- data, experimental or observational design, and comparison condition;
- method in plain language;
- result, effect size or qualitative finding;
- ablation, robustness, or alternative-explanation evidence;
- assumptions required for generalization;
- limitations stated by authors and limitations implied by design.

Keep "what was measured" separate from "what the authors conclude". A benchmark improvement does not by itself prove the broader theory.

Blind spot: can miss conceptual innovation that is not reducible to the evaluation design.

## Concept anatomy lens

Use when a term, distinction, or model is the main object and the user needs to know what it makes visible.

Do not force a fixed list of philosophical dimensions. Choose only dimensions that change understanding, such as:

- origin and problem context;
- neighboring terms and exclusion boundary;
- internal components or formal structure;
- observable examples and counterexamples;
- mechanism or causal role;
- scale and time behavior;
- practical consequences;
- unresolved ambiguity.

End with a compact formulation that preserves the concept's boundary and one falsifying case.

Blind spot: dimensional completeness can become a catalog unless one distinction organizes the result.

## Vertical drilling lens

Use when the surface claim is familiar but its mechanism or premise remains unclear.

Drill through as many supported levels as the evidence permits:

1. observed phenomenon
2. immediate mechanism
3. enabling constraint or principle
4. irreducible assumption, measurement limit, or honest uncertainty

At every level ask: what evidence licenses moving down one layer? Stop when the next layer would require general knowledge not present in the source set.

Blind spot: can turn a contingent historical explanation into a false universal principle.

## Lens selection check

Before reading, record internally:

- lead lens and why it matches the user's question;
- optional secondary lens and the evidence gap it covers;
- one likely blind spot;
- what would cause a lens switch during reading.

After the Source Brief, verify that the lens changed what was extracted. If the same notes would have been produced under every lens, the selection was decorative.
